Subtracting Binary Numbers

Binary subtraction works a lot like decimal subtraction — except it only uses the digits 0 and 1. When subtraction isn't possible directly, we borrow — just like we do in base-10 — but the borrowing rules are slightly different in binary.

Binary Subtraction Rules

Here are the key combinations to remember:

  • 0 − 0 = 0
  • 1 − 0 = 1
  • 1 − 1 = 0
  • 0 − 1 = 1 (borrow required)

Understanding Borrowing

In decimal, we borrow a 10 when subtracting something larger than the current digit. In binary, we borrow a 2, which is written as 10. That’s why when subtracting 1 from 0, we “borrow” and turn it into 10.

Example: Subtract 0110 (6) from 1001 (9)

  1001
- 0110
-------
  1. 1 − 0 = 1
  2. 0 − 1 → borrow → 10 − 1 = 1
  3. 0 − 1 → borrow again → 10 − 1 = 1
  4. 1 − 0 = 1

Final result: 0011

More Complex Borrowing

Sometimes, borrowing requires reaching multiple places to the left before finding a 1. In such cases, each borrow turns into a 10, and the position it was borrowed from becomes 0. It's just like “chaining” your borrowing in decimal subtraction.

Always remember: to verify your binary subtraction, just convert both binary numbers to decimal, subtract, and then check if the binary result equals the expected decimal result.
